And yet, sitting here alone at 32, another relationship dead, another year without a partner, another holiday season explaining to family why I'm still single... can't help but wonder. Grandma Dorothy spent 61 years with someone. Even if that someone didn't really know her, even if she had to hide herself, she was never alone. I'm completely myself, completely free, completely alone. Which is better? Still don't know. Maybe there's no good answer. Maybe both choices involve loss. She lost herself to gain stability. I lose stability to keep myself. Look at Grandma's diary one more time. Her last entry: "I hope they choose themselves sometimes." Sometimes. Not always. Sometimes. Maybe that's the middle ground. Choose yourself sometimes. Compromise sometimes. Be independent sometimes. Be flexible sometimes. But how do you know which times are which? When is compromise healthy and when is it subjugation? When is independence strength and when is it stubbornness? Grandma Dorothy never got to ask those questions. I ask them constantly. Still don't have answers. Maybe that's progress. Or maybe I'm just overthinking what she had to underthink. Phone buzzes. Jessica texting: "Told Todd I'd consider relocating, but only if it makes sense for both our careers. He agreed to make it a joint decision. Feel good about it." Type back: "Proud of you." Mean it, but also feel a twinge of something. Fear, jealousy, worry that she'll disappear like Grandma Dorothy, or worry that she'll find the balance I can't seem to find. Close Grandma's diary. Put it on my bookshelf next to my law books. Two different lives, two different sets of choices, two different kinds of loneliness. Hers: lonely within a marriage. Mine: lonely outside of one. Not sure which is worse. Just know I'm horrified by what marriage cost her and confused by what it might cost me. No, I can't go back to her world, but don't know how to make mine work either. Pour one more glass of wine. Toast to Grandma Dorothy, who stayed, and to myself, who keeps leaving. Both of us trying to figure out how to be loved. Both of us failing in different ways. She got 61 years. I've got nothing but principles and an empty bed. Is that winning? Sitting here in my $900,000 condo that she never could have afforded, with my $240,000 salary she never could have earned, with my law degree she never could have gotten, completely alone in ways she never was. Still don't know if progress means happiness or just different kinds of pain. But I know I can't unknow what I learned. Can't unsee her letters. Can't unread her diary. Can't unhear Grandpa Frank saying "what she wanted didn't matter." Horrified by their marriage. Horrified by my own failures. Horrified that both feel like losing. Guess that's the real horror: Not that the past was bad, but that the present isn't clearly better—just different. Grandma Dorothy and I: two sides of the same coin. She sacrificed everything for stability. I sacrificed stability for everything. Both of us wondering if we chose wrong. Both of us trapped by our choices—hers by lack of them, mine by abundance of them. Close the diary. Sit in silence. Think about Marcus. Think about all of them. Think about dying alone. Think about Grandma dying having never been herself. Don't know which is worse. Just know I'm sitting here at 32 surrounded by everything she wanted and missing everything she had. And somehow that feels like the saddest thing of all.
